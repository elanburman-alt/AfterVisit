**Opener pattern for this file:** Open by naming the board member who made the introduction — acknowledge the connection before you acknowledge the meeting. This is the "board-intro" pattern.

**Scenario context (synthetic — reference while writing, do not transcribe into the body):**
- Donor: Daniel Park, mid-career executive, no giving history. Introduced by board member Cora Reyes.
- Setting: 30-minute coffee, his choice of spot. Discovery visit only.
- What landed: He volunteered that his father spent six weeks in a rehab unit two years ago and felt the staff "actually listened." Asked a thoughtful question about how the foundation decides which programs to invest in.
- Commitment in the meeting: None expected. Mutual interest in a second conversation.
- Next step this email needs to land: A second meeting in roughly four weeks, this time on-site, with a brief walkthrough of the rehab unit if he's open to it.

---

[Paragraph 1 — opener. Name Cora, thank her implicitly by acknowledging the introduction. One sentence acknowledging the meeting itself. Do not over-thank.]

[Paragraph 2 — mirror back. Name the specific thing he told you about his father, in a way that shows you actually heard it. This is the core of a discovery email. One or two sentences. Do not editorialize about the rehab unit.]

[Paragraph 3 — answer his question honestly. The honest answer about how programs get chosen is more interesting than a polished one. Two or three sentences.]

[Paragraph 4 — the next step. Propose the on-site visit. Frame it as continuing the conversation, not as a tour. Give him an easy out if a walkthrough is too much.]
